Research

My work brings together analytical philosophy of time and social-political theory, asking what role temporal structures play in political life.

Areas of Inquiry

Research Focus

01

Analytical Philosophy of Time

I work within the analytic tradition on debates about the nature of time: the reality of temporal passage, the ontological status of past and future, and the relationship between time, change, and causation. I engage closely with McTaggart's distinction between A- and B-series, and with contemporary responses in the form of presentism, eternalism, and the growing block.

Metaphysics Temporal Ontology McTaggart Presentism
02

Democratic Theory & Temporality

My central project examines how democratic institutions are structured by — and often ill-fitted to — the temporal demands placed on them: short electoral horizons, long-term policy effects, and the special problem of obligations to future generations who cannot participate in today's decisions.

Democracy Deliberation Future Generations Institutions
03

Collective Memory & Political Identity

Communities construct shared pasts that ground political identities and moral claims. I investigate the normative dimensions of collective memory: what obligations arise from historical injustices, who counts as a bearer of inherited responsibility, and what role narratives of the past should play in contemporary politics.

Memory Identity Historical Justice Reconciliation
04

Persistence & Personal Identity

Drawing on debates in metaphysics about how objects and persons persist through time, I examine what it means for a collective agent — a people, a state, a social movement — to remain "the same" over time, and what moral and political consequences follow from continuity of identity.

Personal Identity Collective Agency Persistence
Current Project

Time and Democracy

My book project, Time and Democracy, develops a systematic account of the temporal dimensions of democratic governance. The central argument is that modern democracies suffer from a structural "temporal mismatch" — the mechanisms of democratic accountability are calibrated to short cycles, while the most important consequences of political decisions play out over decades or centuries.

The book draws on analytical philosophy of time to distinguish different types of temporal claims in democratic discourse — claims about past injustices, present urgency, and future obligations — and argues that each type requires a distinct institutional response.

A central chapter addresses the problem of future generations: how can a democracy genuinely represent those who cannot yet vote? I propose a novel procedural framework grounded in the idea of democratic stewardship, drawing on both analytic political philosophy and the continental tradition of political phenomenology.
