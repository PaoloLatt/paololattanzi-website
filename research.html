Research

My work spans analytical philosophy of time, ontology, analytic metaphysics, foundations of physics, and analytical social theory.

Areas of Inquiry

Research Focus

01

Philosophy of Time

I work within the analytic tradition on debates about the nature of time: the reality of temporal passage, the ontological status of past and future, and the relationship between time, change, and causation. I engage closely with McTaggart's A/B-distinction, the Rate Argument, and contemporary debates between presentism, eternalism, and the growing block.

Temporal Ontology McTaggart A/B-Theory Rate Argument
02

Ontology

Investigating the fundamental structure of reality through the methods of analytic ontology. My work examines questions of existence, identity, parthood, and the categories of being — with particular attention to objects, properties, and events as they relate to time.

Metaphysics Existence Identity Categories
03

Analytic Metaphysics

Engaging with the core programme of analytic metaphysics — modal logic, persistence, possible worlds, and the relationship between language, thought, and reality. I am especially interested in the intersection of metaphysics with the natural sciences.

Modal Logic Persistence Possible Worlds
04

Foundations of Physics

Exploring the philosophical implications of fundamental physics — in particular, what special relativity and quantum mechanics reveal about the nature of time, space, and causation. The Twin Paradox and its implications for the metaphysics of temporal passage are a central concern.

Special Relativity Twin Paradox Philosophy of Physics
05

Analytical Social Theory

Applying the methods of analytic philosophy to questions of social structure, political economy, and international relations. My work in this area engages with Marxist systems theory, imperialism, and the structural dynamics of the contemporary world-system.

Marxist Theory IR Theory Systems Theory Political Economy
Current Project

PhD Research at Universität Siegen

My doctoral research, conducted at Universität Siegen since 2023, sits at the intersection of Philosophy of Time, Analytical Metaphysics, and Ontology. The project develops a systematic account of temporal passage and its relationship to physical theory — in particular, to what special relativity tells us about time.

One strand of the research examines the Rate Argument — the question of whether time can coherently be said to "pass" at a rate — and its implications for A-theories of time. I presented early findings at the DGPhil 2024 conference under the title "The Rate Argument and the Twin Paradox."

A second strand investigates the relationship between tense and modality: how the grammatical marking of past, present, and future connects to claims about what is possible and what is necessary. I presented this work at SOPhiA 2023.
