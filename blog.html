Philosophy of Time
DGPhil 2024 — Conference Presentation

The Rate Argument and the Twin Paradox

Can time genuinely pass at a rate — and what does special relativity's famous twin paradox reveal about that rate? If one twin ages more slowly during high-speed travel, does time "pass" at different rates for each? I examine what this tells us about A-theories of temporal passage and whether the Rate Argument can survive relativistic physics.

Tense and Modality

What is the relationship between tense — the grammatical marking of past, present, and future — and modal claims about what is possible and what is necessary? I explore tense logic and its connections to modal logic, arguing that temporal and modal operators share deep structural features that standard treatments overlook.

Analytical Social Theory
On the paper published in Marxism & Sciences, 2026

Towards a Marxist Systems Theory of International Relations

Standard IR theories — realism, liberalism, constructivism — share a common blind spot: they treat the international system as a set of interacting states rather than as a structured totality shaped by capitalist dynamics. Drawing on systems theory and Marxist political economy, I propose a framework centred on the concept of "uneven ties."

Imperialismus heute und der Ukraine-Krieg

The war in Ukraine is not merely a conflict between two states — it is a node in the broader structure of inter-imperialist rivalry. McTaggart's A-Series: Still the Best Game in Town?

McTaggart's 1908 argument that time is unreal, and the A/B distinction that it rests on, remain central to the metaphysics of time more than a century later. I examine why A-theories continue to attract defenders despite the serious challenges posed by physics and the rate argument.

What Relativity Tells Us — and Doesn't Tell Us — About Time

Special relativity is often cited as decisive evidence against A-theories of time. If simultaneity is relative, there can be no objective "now." But does relativity really settle the metaphysical question, or does it merely shift its terms? I argue for a more nuanced reading of what physics can and cannot tell us.
